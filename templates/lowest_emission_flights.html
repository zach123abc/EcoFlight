Lowest emission flights

Choose the flight with the least environmental impact

Why is it important?

In a globalised world where travel has become a routine part of daily life, aviation has become one of the fastest-growing sources of greenhouse gas emissions, currently contributing to 2.4% of global CO₂e emissions. By 2050, this share could increase to 22%.

Beyond CO₂ emitted from burning fuels, aircraft engines also affect the concentration of other atmospheric gases and pollutants. When released at high altitudes, these emissions alter the physical and chemical properties of the atmosphere, potentially forming long-lasting contrail cirrus clouds. When including these non-CO₂ effects, aviation contributed to 4% of the global temperature increase since pre-industrial times.

The climate impact of aviation is projected to rise by 200–360% by 2050, even with maximum use of lower-carbon alternative fuels.

What's the solution?

The solution to reducing carbon emissions in air travel involves several key strategies. First, allowing only economy class significantly lowers CO₂ emissions per passenger due to lighter and more numerous seats and fewer amenities. Second, taking direct flights minimises the fuel-intensive take-off and landing stages. Third, fuel-efficient aircraft offer improvement through advanced technology, greatly reducing in-flight energy use. Finally, choosing more efficient airlines that maximise seating capacity, have high load factors and improve operational efficiency, reduces emissions further.

Point of attention

Radiative forcing: Condensation trails of water vapour generated by planes contribute to the formation of cirrus clouds. Those clouds trap outgoing infrared radiation more effectively than they reflect incoming solar radiation, participating in a warming effect. Studies show their effect is equivalent to double or even triple the CO₂ emissions from aviation — meaning over 60% of aviation's impact on global warming could come from non-CO₂ effects.

Key actions

#1 Fly economy class

Flying business class emits 3 times more CO₂e per passenger than economy class (and 9 times more in first class). Economy class, with smaller, lighter and more numerous seats, means each passenger accounts for a lesser portion of the whole plane's carbon emissions. There are fewer amenities, fewer extra services and less baggage allowance, further reducing the plane's total weight.

#2 Prefer direct flights

Take-off and landing are fuel-intensive. During take-off, aircraft engines operate at maximum thrust, consuming a substantial amount of fuel to gain altitude. Similarly, landing requires considerable fuel to control descent and maintain safe speeds. That is why direct flights significantly reduce emissions compared to connecting routes.

#3 Favour more efficient airlines

Airline efficiency is determined by factors such as aircraft fuel burn, engine type, winglets, seating and cargo capacity, passenger load factor, and freight share of total payload. Some airlines are managed more efficiently than others, resulting in fewer emissions per passenger. The most critical factors remain aircraft fuel burn and seating density.

#4 Use search engines that show climate impact

Use search engines that provide an estimation of greenhouse gas emissions per flight. For example, the Travel Impact Model provides emissions per seat/passenger for each flight by integrating factors such as origin, destination, aircraft type, cabin class and seat configuration. Google Flights uses this model — but note that non-CO₂ effects are not yet included.

#5 Adapt the booking process

Whether flights are booked directly by staff, through an internal department, or a travel agency, make sure that emissions per flight are showcased and taken into account in the final choice. Adapt the booking process to privilege less-emitting itineraries through both voluntary and compulsory measures — showing lower-emission options first, requiring staff to prefer the least emissive routes.

To consider

Potential co-benefits

  • Cost savings: Efficient airlines often have lower operational costs, leading to more competitive ticket prices. Economy class also offers additional savings.
  • Encouraging sustainability: Favouring efficient airlines creates demand for more sustainable practices across the industry.
  • Health benefits: Reducing air travel emissions decreases particulate matter and noise pollution globally.
  • Improved efficiency: Direct flights reduce travel time, eliminate baggage transfers, and lower the risk of missed connections.

Success conditions

  • Leading by example: Managers and leaders should demonstrate commitment by choosing less emissive flight options.
  • Updated travel policy: The organisation's travel policy must incorporate the preference for low-emission flights and ensure compliance.
  • Budget management: Higher costs of direct flights can be offset by flying economy class or reducing the number of trips.
  • Raising awareness: Engaging leadership in the decision-making process is key to ensuring broader organisational acceptance.

Prerequisites & specific considerations

  • Availability: Direct flights or routes with more efficient airlines may not always be available.
  • Policy integration: Implementing this solution requires adjustments to the travel policy and booking tools.

Potential risks

  • Increased booking time: Selecting more efficient routes or airlines may require additional effort during the booking process.
  • Higher costs: Direct flights are often more expensive than connecting options.
  • Comfort concerns: Some staff may perceive a move from premium to economy class as a loss of benefits.
